Stop solving algorithmic problems by memorizing the solutions. If you try to memorize 500 LeetCode solutions, you'll fail. If you learn 15 underlying **patterns**, you can solve almost anything. Today, we look at the most famous one: the Sliding Window.

## The Concept

The Sliding Window is a technique used to perform operations on a specific window size of a given array or linked list, such as finding the longest subarray containing all 1s.

Instead of computing the properties of the window from scratch at each step (which is often $O(N^2)$), we slide the window by removing the element going out of the window and adding the new element entering it. This reduces the time complexity to $O(N)$.

<Analogy>
Imagine you are looking at a long train through a small window in a fence. You can only see 3 train cars at a time. As the train moves forward, one car exits your view on the left, and a new car enters your view on the right. You don't have to re-examine all 3 cars; you just note what left and what entered.
</Analogy>

## Recognizing the Pattern

You should immediately think of the Sliding Window pattern when you see:
1. The problem involves a **contiguous subarray** or **substring**.
2. You need to find a maximum, minimum, longest, shortest, or specific sum/value.
3. The data structure is a linear structure like an Array, String, or Linked List.

## Fixed vs Dynamic Windows

There are two main variations of this pattern.

### Fixed Window
The size of the window `k` remains constant throughout the entire array.
**Example**: "Find the maximum sum of any contiguous subarray of size `k`."

### Dynamic Window
The size of the window grows or shrinks based on a specific condition.
**Example**: "Find the smallest contiguous subarray whose sum is greater than or equal to `S`."

<Mistake>
A common beginner mistake is using a nested `for` loop to check every single subarray combination. While this works, it results in an $O(N^2)$ time complexity which will cause a Time Limit Exceeded (TLE) error in almost every technical interview.
</Mistake>

## Putting It Together

By keeping track of a `left` and `right` pointer, you define the boundaries of your window. You expand the window by moving `right`, and you shrink it by moving `left` when a condition is violated.
